[Bachelor Thesis] Analyzing Feature Extraction for Automatic Conversation Analysis on 360-Degree Videos

Anna Penzkofer
April, 2020

Abstract

For decades, psychologists have systematically studied human behavior in group interactions as part of the field of social psychology. A core element of these studies is the analysis of conversations between two or more people, with an emphasis on understanding non-verbal behavior, i.e. behavior unrelated to the content of the conversation. Given that an individual’s non-verbal behavior is often unconscious or automatic, this behavior is harder to “fake” and has been shown to reliably reveal individual personality traits. With the advances in computational technology at the end of the last century, the new field of social signal processing (SSP), also known as computational social sciences, has emerged as a leading field in the study of non-verbal behavior. SSP incorporates computers for automatic processing of video and audio data to extract relevant non-verbal features for conversation analysis. These features are then used to analyze and predict group dynamics, such as emergent leaders, group performance and satisfaction, as well as individual personality traits of group members. In the past, SSP researchers relied on developing customized hardware or conducting studies with elaborate multi-sensor setups in order to collect the required datasets, preventing these studies from being conducted in real-life settings. However, with the emergence of affordable, off-the-shelf 360-degree cameras that do not require a special setup and which capture the full horizontal range of a room, it is now possible to inexpensively capture any kind of group interaction. Moreover, recent advances in machine learning, video processing, and computer vision open up new possibilities for the automatic extraction of non-verbal features that have been successfully used to infer individual and group behavior. With this in mind, we developed a novel feature extraction pipeline enabling researchers to track unambiguous interaction characteristics solely from image data. Specifically, after the position of each person is detected and a distinct identity label is assigned, single-person head pose estimation, speaking activity tracking, body movement, and hand movement tracking is performed in parallel for each individual. These features are then evaluated by the automated pipeline to determine their predictive value for certain dynamics of group interaction, as well as individual personality traits. In order to test our new pipeline, we conducted a data collection study in which we recorded four videos of five groups of varying size, featuring a total number of 19 participants. With this new showcase dataset, we present the efficacy of our proposed pipeline. In fact, the pipeline was able to extract three statistically significant features for individual behavior analysis, as well as two statistically significant features related to dynamics of group interaction in conversation analysis. The contribution of this work is three-fold 1) a showcase dataset for group interactions, including dyadic and multi-person conversations taken by a 360-degree camera, 2) a novel processing pipeline for tracking multiple modalities on 360-degree videos, and 3) an evaluation of each feature in relation to its predictive value.
